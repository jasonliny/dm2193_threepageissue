What's the point?

People have a right to privacy. Even if there is nothing malicious being done, it doesn't mean everything needs to be public.

As more and more of our lives revolve around the Internet, it's becoming increasing important to care about privacy both in the sense of keeping sensitive data secure but also keeping personal actions private. This includes information we expect to be private such as direct messages between two people or login information as well as information we may not be as aware of such as the metadata associated with the messages being sent, the exact time and I.P. address associated when logging into a site or system, or even the logging of all of one's search and browsing history.

While this information may not be important to some people, it may be of utter importance for others such as people is situations where it may be used against them (e.g. whistleblowers).
Even in less dangerous circumstances, the collection and use of one's data could have negative impacts. As an example, in the case of collecting browsing data, the data may be used to tailor content to a specific person. One of the drawbacks of targeted content is that it can create a bubble or feedback loop where a person is frequently served the same type of content. This could be especially dangerous when used in a searrch engine since given the same input, two queries may return significantly different results with varying degrees of bias and credibility.

What to do about it?

One of the first steps to take is to identify a threat model

In general, it is infeasible to protect every single bit of data from everyone all the time
Instead, one should outline what is important to them and continue from there.
